Sources of Law  — mindmap

Common Law

  • "Judge-made law"
  • Built case-by-case
  • Originally rigid, fixed remedies

Equity

  • Appeals to the monarch
  • "Fairness & conscience"
  • Merged with common law

Legislation

  • Statutes by legislatures
  • Codifies or creates law
  • e.g. Professional Engineers Act

Regulations

  • Made under a statute
  • Detail/scope (e.g. exams)
  • Valid only if authorized

Constitution

  • Supreme law (s.52)
  • Charter of Rights
  • Overrides inconsistent law

Theory of Precedent (stare decisis)

The rule

Courts follow legal principles from earlier decisions on similar facts → predictability.

The flexibility

Courts may apply equitable discretion or distinguish cases on minor factual differences.

The trade-off

Departures evolve slowly — sometimes criticized, but the basis of a stable system.

Division of Powers — Constitution Act, 1867

Federal — s.91

  • Trade & commerce
  • Taxation (any mode)
  • Navigation & shipping
  • Banking · currency
  • Bankruptcy & insolvency
  • Patents · copyrights
  • Criminal law

Provincial — s.92

  • Direct taxation (prov.)
  • Public lands & timber
  • Local works
  • Incorporating cos.
  • Property & civil rights
  • Admin of justice
  • Local/private matters
"POGG" — Parliament's residual power over Peace, Order & good Government covers anything not listed; read broadly for national concerns. A law beyond a government's authority is ultra vires → void.

The Charter of Rights & Freedoms (Constitution Act, 1982)

What changed

Courts can now strike down laws that violate rights — not just laws made by the wrong level of government. Reduces Parliamentary Supremacy.

s.2 freedoms

Conscience & religion · thought/expression & press · peaceful assembly · association.

The limits

s.1 "reasonable limits" · s.33 notwithstanding clause lets a gov't keep a law despite a violation. s.52: Constitution is supreme.

Two More Distinctions

Public vs Private law

Public: gov't ↔ individuals (criminal, constitutional). Private: individuals ↔ each other (contracts, torts).

Quebec is different

Civil Code (from the Napoleonic Code), not common law. Get advice from a Quebec lawyer. Note: "civil" in common law usually means "private."

Key Terminology — quick recall

Litigation — a lawsuit
Plaintiff — brings the claim (Crown, in criminal)
Defendant — defends the claim ("accused" in criminal)
Appellant — party appealing a decision
Respondent — party upholding the decision
Privity of contract — legal relationship between contract parties
Creditor — owed an amount
Debtor — owes a creditor
Indemnification — promise to directly reimburse a loss; unlike a guarantee, can be claimed directly without first pursuing the debtor
Rule of Law: no one is above the law. Judiciary and legislatures are separate & independent; an independent judiciary enforces laws, protects rights, and (with precedent) makes outcomes predictable for business.
