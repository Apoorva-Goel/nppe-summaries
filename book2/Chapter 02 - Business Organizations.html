The Three Basic Forms — compare at a glance

Sole Proprietorship

  • One individual, for themselves
  • Keeps all profit personally
  • Personally liable for all losses
  • No separate legal entity

Partnership

  • Persons in business "in common, with a view to profit"
  • Share profits & losses personally
  • Strength in numbers — but each partner personally liable for partnership debts
  • Creditors can seize personal assets

Corporation

  • Separate entity — a "fictitious person"
  • Owns its own assets, owes its own debts
  • Sues / is sued in its own name
  • Shareholders not liable for its debts

Independence of the Corporate Entity

The foundational case

Salomon v Salomon & Co (1897, HL): a corporation is legally distinct from its shareholders. Limited liability is a deliberate incentive to invest. Still fundamental in Canadian law.

"Lifting the corporate veil"

Courts will ignore the separate entity where it's used to perpetrate a fraud, or in other exceptional cases (e.g. Nedco v Clark, 1973). The veil is the exception, not the rule.

Duration, Guarantees & Tax

Duration

A partnership can dissolve on a partner's death/withdrawal (unless the agreement says otherwise). A corporation has continuous existence.

Personal guarantees

A shareholder isn't liable for corporate debts in theory — but lenders often require a personal guarantee, restoring personal exposure.

Tax basics

Corporate income taxed at corporate rates; dividends to shareholders taxed again personally — the two layers of tax to weigh.

Engineer's takeaway: choice of form = a trade-off between simplicity and personal liability protection. The corporation shields personal assets — but personal guarantees and the fraud exception can pierce that shield.
