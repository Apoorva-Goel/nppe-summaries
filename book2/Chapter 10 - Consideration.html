Consideration

The "price" of a promise — what each side gives to make a bargain enforceable.
Mutual exchangeAdequacySealed documentsEquitable estoppel

What Consideration Is

The rule

Each party must give something of value (a promise or act). No consideration → no contract.

Adequacy

Courts don't police whether the deal is fair — even nominal value can suffice. They care that consideration exists, not that it's equal.

The seal

A sealed document is enforceable without consideration — important in tendering (the bid bond / sealed tender).

Equitable Estoppel — the exception

A purely gratuitous promise (no consideration) normally can't be enforced. But where one party, by word or conduct, makes a promise intended to affect legal relations and the other relies on it, equity may stop the promisor from going back on it.

Key cases: Conwest Exploration v Letain (SCC 1963) · John Burrows v Subsurface Surveys (SCC) — estoppel needs a genuine course of negotiation/conduct, not mere indulgence (e.g. accepting late payments) · Owen Sound Library v Mial Developments (Ont CA 1979).

Estoppel is a shield, not a sword: it usually defends against enforcement of strict rights — it doesn't on its own create a new cause of action.
