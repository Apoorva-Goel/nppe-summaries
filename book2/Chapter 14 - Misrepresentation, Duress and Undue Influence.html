Three Vitiating Factors — mindmap

Misrepresentation

  • A false statement of fact
  • Induces the contract

Duress

  • Contract induced by intimidation/threats
  • Makes it voidable

Undue Influence

  • Improper pressure from a position of power/trust
  • Consent not truly free

Misrepresentation: Innocent vs Fraudulent

Innocent

False assertion made by someone who doesn't realize it's false. Remedy: rescission (unwind the contract).

Fraudulent — Derry v Peek

A statement made (1) knowingly, (2) without belief in its truth, or (3) recklessly. Remedy: rescission + damages for reasonable costs.

Engineering specs: contractors rely on plans/specs, but contracts often state that engineers' estimates are not binding on the owner. An honest, mistaken estimate is not deceit (no fraud without knowledge/recklessness).

Economic Duress — Gotaverken v Cariboo (BCSC 1993)

A contractor, mid-project, threatened to slow work unless a fixed-price contract was changed to time-and-materials. With no other firm realistically available to finish, the owner agreed — to its economic detriment. The court found economic duress: a party forced to renegotiate to its disadvantage under illegitimate pressure can have the amended deal set aside.
