Contract Interpretation

When parties dispute what the contract means, how do courts decide?

Three Interpretation Tools

Liberal vs strict

Courts may read a contract liberally (purpose & commercial sense) or strictly (plain words). SCC in Hunter Engineering v Syncrude (1989) leaned to the liberal approach.

Contra proferentem

Ambiguity is construed against the party that drafted the clause. A powerful incentive to draft clearly.

Parol evidence rule

Outside evidence generally can't add to or vary a written contract — with exceptions (e.g. to show the contract never truly existed, or for collateral terms).

Implied Terms

Courts will imply an obvious omitted term only where it's necessary to give the contract business efficacy — not merely because it would be reasonable (Pigott Construction v W J Crowe, Ont CA 1961; G Ford Homes v Draft Masonry, Ont CA 1983).

Engineer's takeaway: the best defence against an interpretation dispute is clear, unambiguous drafting. Put all agreed terms in the written contract — contra proferentem and the parol evidence rule both reward clarity.
