Legality

A contract for an unlawful purpose won't be enforced — whether it offends a statute or public policy.
Contrary to statuteContrary to common lawPublic policyNon-competition

Two Routes to Illegality — mindmap

Contrary to Statute Law

  • Breaches Bankruptcy & Insolvency Act (e.g. transfers between related parties)
  • Breaches the Competition Act (lessening competition)
  • Waives lien rights contrary to the Construction Act
  • Services needing a licence performed by an unlicensed party

Contrary to Common Law / Public Policy

  • Provisions against public policy are void
  • Classic example: non-competition / restraint of trade
  • Tested for reasonableness in scope, time & geography

The Unlicensed-Contractor Cases

Kocotis v D'Angelo (Ont CA 1958)

Unlicensed electrician couldn't recover — by-law existed to protect the public from unqualified work.

Calax v Lepofsky (1974)

Followed Kocotis — unlicensed building contractor denied recovery.

Monticchio v Torcema (1979)

A departure — signalled courts may allow compensation despite the licensing defect in some cases.

Non-competition test: the party enforcing a restraint must show the terms are reasonable — between the parties and in the public interest. Over-broad clauses (too long, too wide) are struck as restraint of trade.
