Benefiting from Technical Societies

Voluntary organizations that advance knowledge — distinct from the mandatory licensing Associations.
Knowledge sharingVoluntaryTypes of societyEIC

Why They Matter

For ~two centuries, technical societies have encouraged research, collected & classified information, and disseminated it to members and the public — the technical equivalent of learned societies in the arts. In countries without strict licensing, they may also offer voluntary certification.

Society ≠ Association: a licensing Association (PEO, APEGA…) is the mandatory regulator that grants your right to practise. A technical society is a voluntary body for knowledge, networking & professional development. Don't confuse the two.

Types of Technical Society

Discipline-oriented

Organized around a field (e.g. geotechnical, chemical).

Geographical

Organized by region.

Federations

Umbrella bodies of member societies (e.g. EIC).

Government / industrial

Tied to an employer or sector.

Canadian examples: Engineering Institute of Canada (EIC) — a federation; Canadian Geotechnical Society; Chemical Institute of Canada (CIC). First Canadian society: Engineering Society of the University of Toronto (1885).

Engineer's takeaway: join at least one technical society to maintain your technical interests, networks, and CPD (Ch 17). There's a society for every discipline, industry, and region — and it benefits from your participation too.
