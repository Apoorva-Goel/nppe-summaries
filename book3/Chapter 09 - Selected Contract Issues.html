Two recurring issues for design professionals: who can bind whom (agency), and who absorbs a loss (indemnity).

Agency & Authority

Express authority

Authority the principal actually grants the agent (e.g. CCDC 2: "the Consultant has authority to act for the Owner only to the extent provided in the Contract Documents").

Apparent (ostensible) authority

Created by the principal's representations/conduct that the agent may act. The principal is then bound — even absent an actual agreement.

Critical rule: apparent authority must come from the principal, not the agent. An agent saying "I have authority" is not enough. If someone holds themselves out as an agent with no authority, the supposed principal isn't bound. Also beware imputed knowledge — what the agent knows is treated as known by the principal.

Indemnities

An indemnity is an agreement by one party to bear another's financial loss for a specified event. Common in construction (e.g. the contractor indemnifies the owner & consultant against claims for bodily injury, death, or property damage arising from the work). Read the trigger and scope carefully — indemnities can shift very large liabilities.

Engineer's takeaway: confirm a signatory's actual authority before relying on their commitments, and scrutinize every indemnity/hold-harmless clause — it decides who pays when something goes wrong.
