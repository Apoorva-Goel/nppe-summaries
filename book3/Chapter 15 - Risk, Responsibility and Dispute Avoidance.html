Risk, Responsibility & Dispute Avoidance

A contract's ultimate purpose is to allocate risk — what you don't assign, a court will assign for you.
Risk allocationRisk assessmentCommon-law presumptionsDuty to report errors

The Core Idea

Every contract allocates risk and responsibility between the parties. Anything left unaddressed falls where the courts decide it falls — usually not where you'd have chosen. Naming risks up front is the cheapest insurance there is.

Ask the Right Questions — risk assessment

Sample questions

What loss results from each day of delay? Who bears the risk the technology doesn't work? What if the challenge is harder than expected? Who finishes if a provider defaults?

Why it works

Simply asking these questions surfaces logical solutions before they become disputes. Dissatisfaction is the seed of conflict — pre-empt it.

Common-Law Presumptions

Sophisticated parties

Courts treat professionals as sophisticated — and presume an identified risk was consciously addressed.

No guarantee of result

The law does not presume a professional guarantees the outcome — only reasonable skill & care.

Implied warranties

Some warranties may be implied — but contract terms can modify or exclude them (e.g. contractor's duty to report errors).

Engineer's takeaway: don't rely on the default rules — allocate every material risk expressly. Clauses like "the Contractor shall promptly report any error/inconsistency/omission" deliberately shift the common-law position.
