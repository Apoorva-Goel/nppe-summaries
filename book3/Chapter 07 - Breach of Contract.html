Four Reasons Contracts Are Breached — mindmap

Inability

  • Wants to perform but can't
  • e.g. lack of funds/resources

Inadvertence

  • Unintentional failure
  • Overlooked an obligation

Disagreement

  • Parties read the contract differently
  • e.g. Peter Kiewit v Eakins

Anticipated losses

  • Cheaper to breach than perform
  • Owners guard with performance bonds
Performance under protest: where obligations are disputed, a party may perform under protest (with proper notice) and pursue the claim later — common in standard-form construction contracts. It avoids being trapped between breaching and waiving the dispute.

Damages — the Three Limitations

1 · Causation

The loss must have been caused by the breach.

2 · Remoteness

The loss must have been reasonably foreseeable (Hadley v Baxendale principle) — not too remote.

3 · Mitigation

The innocent party must take reasonable steps to reduce its loss.

Remedy reality: by far the most common remedy is damages (money). Equitable remedies like specific performance or injunctions are exceptional. Damages aim to put the innocent party where it would have been had the contract been performed.
