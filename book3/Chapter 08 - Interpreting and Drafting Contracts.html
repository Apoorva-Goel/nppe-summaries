Interpreting & Drafting Contracts

When words are disputed, courts follow a hierarchy of principles — none of which let them rewrite the deal.
Objective intentionCanons of constructionContra proferentemAdditional evidence

The Overriding Principle

The court's goal is the reasonable mutual (objective) intention of the parties — what the words would mean to a reasonable person. The court will not rewrite the contract or supply one the parties failed to make.

The Canons of Construction — guides, not rules of law

Plain & ordinary meaning

The "golden rule" — words get their grammatical, ordinary sense.

Special/technical meaning

Technical terms (common in construction) take their customary trade meaning.

Read as a whole

Interpret each clause in light of the entire contract.

Give effect to all parts

Avoid readings that make any provision meaningless.

Express provisions govern

Special/supplemental conditions override the standard form.

Commercial purpose & context

Prefer the most commercially reasonable result; meaning depends on context.

Two Decisive Rules

Contra proferentem

If a clause is genuinely ambiguous, it's read against the drafter.

Additional evidence

The contract itself is primary evidence. Where it's clear and unambiguous, courts generally won't admit outside evidence to vary it.

Drafting takeaway: ambiguity always costs the drafter. Define technical terms, make special conditions clearly override the standard form, and say what you mean — clarity beats litigation.
