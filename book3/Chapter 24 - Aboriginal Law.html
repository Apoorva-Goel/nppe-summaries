The Duty to Consult — Haida & Taku River

The Supreme Court held that governments have a duty to consult and accommodate Aboriginal interests when contemplating conduct that could affect them. The degree of consultation scales with the strength of the claim and the severity of the impact.

Key limit: the duty to consult/accommodate falls on government — not private parties. But projects can stall if consultation is inadequate, so professionals must factor it into planning.

Practical Issues for Projects

Reserve land

Generally unpatented Crown land — so builders' liens and mortgages cannot attach to it. Securing payment differs.

Project participation

Advantages (e.g. tax exemptions) but disadvantages too — can't mortgage reserve land, and collection against property is difficult.

Band councils

Not incorporated entities — their legal status (and capacity to contract / be sued) has been debated. Confirm authority carefully.

Engineer's takeaway: on projects involving Aboriginal land or interests, expect a government duty to consult, and remember that normal payment security (liens/mortgages) may not work on reserve land — structure contracts accordingly.
